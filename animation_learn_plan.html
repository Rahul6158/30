30-Day Paper Animation Challenge

Master 2D animation fundamentals using just pencil and paper - no digital tools required

Week 1: Core Principles

1

Introduction to Animation

Discover the magic behind bringing drawings to life.

Watch & Learn

The Illusion of Life (Pixar) History of Animation
Paper Exercise

Flipbook Test: Create a 20-page flipbook showing a simple shape moving across the page. Focus on making the movement smooth and consistent between pages.

2

Squash and Stretch

Give your animations weight and flexibility.

Watch & Learn

Squash and Stretch Explained Real-world Examples
Paper Exercise

Ball Bounce: Draw a ball bouncing with proper squash (when it hits the ground) and stretch (when it's falling/rising). Create 5 key drawings showing the full motion.

3

Timing and Spacing

Control the rhythm and speed of your animations.

Watch & Learn

Timing Basics Spacing Charts
Paper Exercise

Timing Chart: Create a timing chart for a pendulum swing showing slow-in/slow-out with at least 7 drawings. Label the keyframes (1), breakdowns (×), and inbetweens (2,3,etc.).

4

Anticipation

Prepare your audience for upcoming actions.

Watch & Learn

Anticipation Principle Examples in Cartoons
Paper Exercise

Jump Anticipation: Draw 3 key poses showing anticipation before a jump: 1) Standing, 2) Squatting down (anticipation), 3) Full extension upward.

5

Follow Through

Make movements feel natural with overlapping action.

Watch & Learn

Follow Through Explained Hair and Clothing Demo
Paper Exercise

Dog Tail Wag: Draw 5 key poses of a dog wagging its tail, showing how the tail movement follows through after the body stops.

6

Slow In & Out

Create realistic acceleration and deceleration.

Watch & Learn

Easing Explained Practical Examples
Paper Exercise

Ball Roll: Draw a ball rolling to a stop with proper slow out (more drawings at the end). Show the spacing getting closer together as it slows.

7

Week 1 Review

Combine all principles into one exercise.

Watch & Learn

12 Principles Recap
Paper Exercise

Bouncing Ball with Personality: Animate a bouncing ball showing anticipation before a big bounce, squash/stretch on impact, and slow in/out. Add follow-through if it has a tail or ears.

Week 2: Movement & Mechanics

8

Arcs and Paths

Natural movement follows curved paths.

Watch & Learn

Arcs Principle Finding Arcs in Nature
Paper Exercise

Arc Diagrams: Draw the arcs for: 1) A pendulum swing, 2) A bird flying, 3) An arm waving. Then animate a simple object moving along an arc path.

9

Secondary Action

Add richness with supporting movements.

Watch & Learn

Secondary Action Explained Character Examples
Paper Exercise

Character Blink: Draw a simple character walking, then add a blink as secondary action. Make sure the blink doesn't distract from the main walk.

10

Exaggeration

Push movements beyond reality for appeal.

Watch & Learn

Exaggeration Principle Cartoon vs. Realistic
Paper Exercise

Pushed Expressions: Draw 3 facial expressions (happy, sad, surprised) first realistically, then with extreme cartoon exaggeration.

11

Solid Drawing

Create volume and weight in drawings.

Watch & Learn

Solid Drawing Basics 3D Form Practice
Paper Exercise

Basic Shapes: Draw cubes, spheres and cylinders from different angles, then animate a simple shape turning in 3D space (5 drawings).

12

Appeal

Create charismatic, interesting designs.

Watch & Learn

Appeal in Design Character Design Tips
Paper Exercise

Silhouette Test: Design 3 simple characters focusing only on their silhouettes. Then refine one with appealing details.

13

Weight & Balance

Make objects feel heavy or light.

Watch & Learn

Weight Demonstration Character Weight
Paper Exercise

Lifting Objects: Draw a character lifting 3 different objects (feather, box, heavy rock) showing appropriate weight through body language.

14

Week 2 Review

Combine all principles into one exercise.

Watch & Learn

Analyzing Animation
Paper Exercise

Character Jump: Animate a character jumping over an obstacle showing: anticipation, squash/stretch, arcs, follow-through, and proper weight. Use at least 8 key drawings.

Week 3: Character Animation

15

Walk Cycles

The foundation of character movement.

Watch & Learn

Walk Cycle Basics Key Poses
Paper Exercise

Basic Walk: Draw the 4 key poses of a walk cycle (contact, down, passing, up) for a simple character. Add inbetweens if time allows.

16

Personality Walks

How walks express character.

Watch & Learn

Walk Variations Cartoon Walks
Paper Exercise

Character Walks: Draw the same character walking in 3 different moods (happy, tired, sneaky). Focus on posture and timing differences.

17

Run Cycles

Faster movement with more extremes.

Watch & Learn

Run vs. Walk Run Key Poses
Paper Exercise

Basic Run: Draw the 3 key poses of a run (contact, airborne, recovery) for your character. Note the lean forward and extended limbs.

18

Facial Expressions

Conveying emotion through the face.

Watch & Learn

Expression Basics Cartoon Expressions
Paper Exercise

Emotion Transition: Draw a character's face changing from happy to sad in 5 steps, focusing on smooth shape changes.

19

Lip Sync Basics

Matching mouth shapes to sounds.

Watch & Learn

Phoneme Shapes Simple Lip Sync
Paper Exercise

Mouth Chart: Draw the basic mouth positions for "Hello" (Neutral, H, E, L, O). Then animate a simple face saying "Hi" in 5 drawings.

20

Gesture Drawing

Capturing dynamic poses quickly.

Watch & Learn

Gesture Basics 30-Second Poses
Paper Exercise

Quick Sketches: Do 10 gesture drawings (30 seconds each) of people or characters in action poses. Focus on flow and energy, not details.

21

Week 3 Review

Combine character animation skills.

Watch & Learn

Character Animation Demo
Paper Exercise

Character Performance: Animate a character reacting to something off-screen (surprise, then reaction). Include facial expressions and body language (minimum 10 drawings).

Week 4: Storytelling & Scenes

22

Staging & Composition

Directing the viewer's eye.

Watch & Learn

Staging Principle Film Composition
Paper Exercise

Scene Layout: Draw 3 different compositions for the same scene (character finding a key). Experiment with camera angles and character placement.

23

Storyboarding Basics

Planning visual sequences.

Watch & Learn

Storyboard Intro Shot Types
Paper Exercise

3-Panel Story: Create a simple 3-panel storyboard showing: 1) Character sees something, 2) Reacts, 3) Takes action. Add basic notes about timing.

24

Thumbnailing Actions

Planning movement sequences.

Watch & Learn

Thumbnail Process Action Planning
Paper Exercise

Action Sequence: Create 5 thumbnail sketches planning out a character picking up a heavy box. Focus on key poses and timing.

25

Scene Timing

Pacing for storytelling.

Watch & Learn

Timing for Emotion Beat Planning
Paper Exercise

Timing Sheet: Plan out a 10-second scene (character hears noise, looks, reacts) using a timing sheet with key actions marked in seconds.

26

Keyframe Drawing

Identifying the most important poses.

Watch & Learn

Key Poses Strong Posing
Paper Exercise

Key Pose Test: For a simple action (opening a door), draw only the 3 most important key poses that define the action.

27

Breakdowns & Inbetweens

Filling in the movement between keys.

Watch & Learn

Inbetweening Smooth Transitions
Paper Exercise

Pose Transition: Draw 2 key poses (character standing and sitting), then add 1 breakdown and 2 inbetweens to smooth the action.

28

Final Project Planning

Developing your short animation.

Watch & Learn

Animation Process
Paper Exercise

Concept Development: Plan your final project: 1) Write a simple premise (1-2 sentences), 2) List key actions, 3) Sketch 3 important poses.

29

Final Thumbnails

Visualizing your entire scene.

Watch & Learn

Scene Planning
Paper Exercise

Full Thumbnail Pass: Create thumbnail sketches for your entire animation (15-30 drawings). Mark timing notes where actions occur.

30

Final Completion

Bringing it all together!

Watch & Learn

12 Principles Recap
Paper Exercise

Final Animation: Complete your short paper animation (5-15 seconds). Present as: 1) Clean keyframe drawings, 2) Timing chart, 3) Flipbook or storyboard.

🎉 Congratulations on completing the 30-Day Paper Animation Challenge! 🎉

Remember: Animation is a lifelong learning process. Keep practicing with these fundamentals.
